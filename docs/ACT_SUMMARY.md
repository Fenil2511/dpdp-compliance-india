# Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023
## Complete Structured Summary — All Chapters & Sections

> **Official Citation**: The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (No. 22 of 2023)
> **President's Assent**: 11th August 2023
> **Published**: Gazette of India, Extraordinary, Part II — Section 1, No. 25
> **Commencement**: On dates notified by Central Government (different dates for different provisions)

---

## CHAPTER I — PRELIMINARY (Sections 1–3)

### Section 1 — Short Title and Commencement
- The Act is titled the **Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023**
- Comes into force on the date(s) the Central Government notifies in the Official Gazette
- **Different provisions may be notified on different dates** — critical for compliance timelines
- Reference to "commencement of this Act" in any provision = coming into force of that provision

---

### Section 2 — Definitions (28 key terms)

| Term | Plain-English Meaning |
|------|----------------------|
| **Appellate Tribunal** | TDSAT — Telecom Disputes Settlement and Appellate Tribunal (established under TRAI Act 1997) |
| **Automated** | Any digital process operating automatically in response to instructions |
| **Board** | Data Protection Board of India (DPBI) — the enforcement regulator |
| **Certain Legitimate Uses** | The 8 processing grounds listed in Section 7 (no consent needed) |
| **Chairperson** | Chairperson of the Data Protection Board of India |
| **Child** | Individual who has NOT completed **18 years** of age |
| **Consent Manager** | A Board-registered entity acting as single point of contact for a Data Principal to give, manage, review and withdraw consent via an accessible, transparent, interoperable platform |
| **Data** | Representation of information, facts, concepts, opinions or instructions processable by humans or automated means |
| **Data Fiduciary (DF)** | Any person who — alone or jointly — **determines the purpose AND means** of processing personal data |
| **Data Principal (DP)** | The individual to whom the personal data relates. If the individual is a child → includes parents/lawful guardian. If deceased/incapacitated → includes nominee |
| **Data Processor** | Any person who processes personal data **on behalf of** a Data Fiduciary |
| **Member** | Member of the Board — includes Chairperson |
| **Notification / Notify / Notified** | Publication in the Official Gazette |
| **Person** | Individual, HUF, company, firm, association of persons, body of individuals (incorporated or not), the State, or any artificial juristic person |
| **Personal Data** | Any data about an individual who is **identifiable by or in relation to** such data |
| **Personal Data Breach** | Unauthorised processing OR accidental disclosure, acquisition, sharing, use, alteration, destruction or **loss of access** to personal data that compromises **CIA** (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability) |
| **Prescribed** | Prescribed by Rules made under this Act |
| **Processing** | Wholly or partly automated operation(s) on digital personal data — including collection, recording, organisation, structuring, storage, adaptation, retrieval, use, alignment, combination, indexing, sharing, disclosure, dissemination, restriction, erasure, destruction |
| **Significant Data Fiduciary (SDF)** | A Data Fiduciary notified by the Central Government under Section 10(1) |
| **State** | Includes Union of India, State Governments, local authorities, other bodies under Government control |

> **Compliance Note**: The definition of "processing" is extremely broad. Merely storing personal data = processing. Sharing with a vendor = processing. Both trigger DPDP obligations.

---

### Section 3 — Applicability and Scope

**The Act APPLIES to:**
1. Processing of **digital personal data** within India (whether collected online or digitised offline)
2. Processing of digital personal data **outside India** — if the processing is in connection with **offering goods or services** to Data Principals located in India

**The Act does NOT apply to:**
1. Personal data processed by an individual for **personal or domestic purposes**
2. Personal data **made publicly available** by:
   - The Data Principal themselves (e.g., public social media posts), OR
   - Any person under a legal obligation to make such data publicly available

> **Illustration (from Act)**: X blogs her views and makes her personal data public on social media. The Act does not apply to that publicly shared data.

> **Critical**: Even if data was collected before the Act, obligations under Section 5 (Notice) apply as soon as practicable after commencement.

## CHAPTER II — OBLIGATIONS OF DATA FIDUCIARY (Sections 4–13)

### Section 4 — Grounds for Processing Personal Data

A Data Fiduciary may process personal data **only** for a lawful purpose and only if:
- **(a) Consent** — the Data Principal has given consent; OR
- **(b) Certain Legitimate Uses** — processing falls under Section 7 grounds

**"Lawful purpose"** = any purpose not expressly forbidden by law.

> **Compliance implication**: Every data collection/processing activity needs a documented legal basis. "We've always collected this" is not a valid basis.

---

### Section 5 — Notice Requirement

Before or alongside requesting consent, the Data Fiduciary must provide a notice containing:
1. **What personal data** will be processed
2. **Purpose** for which it will be processed
3. **How to exercise rights** — specifically rights under Section 6(4) [withdrawal] and Section 13 [grievance]
4. **How to complain to the Board**

**Language**: Notice must be available in **English OR any language specified in the Eighth Schedule to the Constitution** (22 official languages).

**Pre-existing consent** (before Act commencement):
- Data Fiduciary must give notice **as soon as practicable**
- Can continue processing until Data Principal withdraws consent
- Illustration: E-commerce company X had consent from user Z before commencement. Must notify Z via email/in-app/other effective method describing data and processing purpose.

---

### Section 6 — Consent

**Standards for valid consent — ALL must be met:**
- **Free** — not obtained through coercion, deception, or undue influence
- **Specific** — for a particular, specified purpose (not blanket)
- **Informed** — Data Principal understands what they are consenting to
- **Unconditional** — not bundled with other consents
- **Unambiguous** — clear affirmative action (no pre-ticked boxes)
- **Limited to necessary data** — data minimisation principle

**Illustration**: Telemedicine app Y requests consent for (i) telemedicine services and (ii) accessing contact list. Contact list is not necessary for telemedicine → consent for contact list access is invalid under data minimisation.

**Consent Withdrawal (Section 6(4)):**
- Data Principal may withdraw consent at any time
- Withdrawal must be as easy as giving consent
- On withdrawal: DF must stop processing AND instruct all Data Processors to stop
- Data Fiduciary cannot penalise withdrawal (e.g., deny service for legitimate purposes)

**Consent Manager (Sections 6(7)–(9)):**
- Data Principal may give/manage/review/withdraw consent through a Consent Manager
- Consent Manager is accountable to the Data Principal
- Must be registered with the Board (conditions: technical, operational, financial)

**Burden of proof (Section 6(10)):**
- If consent is disputed before the Board, the **Data Fiduciary must prove** notice was given

---

### Section 7 — Certain Legitimate Uses (Processing Without Consent)

These 8 situations allow processing **without consent**:

| # | Ground | Example |
|---|--------|---------|
| 1 | Voluntary provision of data for a specific service (purpose is clear) | User fills a form knowing it's for warranty registration |
| 2 | State or instrumentality of State — function under law, sovereignty, security of State | Government processing tax data |
| 3 | Legal obligation to disclose to State/instrumentality | Bank reporting suspicious transactions to FIU |
| 4 | Compliance with court judgment/decree/order (India or civil/contractual from outside India) | Court-ordered disclosure |
| 5 | **Medical emergency** — threat to life or immediate threat to health of DP or any other individual | Hospital accessing data to treat unconscious patient |
| 6 | Medical treatment/health services during **epidemic, disease outbreak, or public health threat** | Contact tracing during pandemic |
| 7 | Disaster, breakdown of public order | Search and rescue operations |
| 8 | **Employment purposes** — processing necessary for employment-related obligations | Payroll, performance, benefits |

> **Key point**: Even when using a legitimate use ground, the DF must still ensure data security, minimisation, and purpose limitation.

---

### Section 8 — General Obligations of Data Fiduciary

The Data Fiduciary must:

**(1)–(2) Process only for specified purpose** — with valid consent or legitimate use

**(3) Accuracy** — ensure personal data is accurate and complete, especially where:
- It will be used to make a decision affecting the Data Principal
- It will be shared with another DF

**(4) Data minimisation** — collect only data necessary for the specified purpose

**(5) Security safeguards** — implement appropriate technical and organisational measures to protect personal data from breach. Must be proportionate to the nature and volume of data. *(This is the ₹250 crore penalty provision)*

**(6) Breach notification** — upon becoming aware of a personal data breach:
- Notify the **Board** in prescribed manner
- Notify **affected Data Principals** in prescribed manner
- *(See Rule 7 for detailed procedure)*

**(7) Storage limitation / Data erasure** — erase personal data (and cause Data Processors to erase) when:
- **(a)** The specified purpose is no longer being served (consent-based processing), OR
- **(b)** Data Principal withdraws consent

**Exceptions to erasure**: Retention required by law (e.g., RBI KYC retention, Companies Act records)

**Illustration**: X closes her bank account. Bank Y must retain KYC data for 10 years (RBI requirement) → Y is exempt from erasure obligation for that data.

**Purpose deemed no longer served** — if Data Principal:
- Does not approach the DF for the service
- Does not use the service
- …for a period specified by the Central Government

**(8) Data Processor control** — Data Fiduciary is responsible for ensuring Data Processor compliance. DF must have written contracts with Data Processors specifying security and processing obligations.

---

### Section 9 — Processing Personal Data of Children

**Age threshold**: Child = under 18 years

**Requirements before processing:**
1. Obtain **verifiable consent from parent or lawful guardian**
2. Implement age verification mechanisms (Rules prescribe standards)
3. Verify that the consenting person is actually the child's parent/guardian

**Absolute prohibitions — regardless of parental consent:**
- Processing likely to cause **detrimental effect** on the child's well-being
- **Behavioural monitoring** of children
- **Targeted advertising** directed at children

**Exemptions** (to be notified by Central Government):
- Categories of Data Fiduciaries exempt from parental consent (e.g., health/education services for child welfare)
- These will be notified via Official Gazette

> **Penalty**: Up to **₹200 crore** for violations of Section 9.

---

### Section 10 — Additional Obligations of Significant Data Fiduciary

**Who is an SDF?** — Notified by Central Government based on:
- Volume and sensitivity of personal data processed
- Risk to the rights of Data Principals
- Potential impact on sovereignty and integrity of India
- Risk to electoral democracy
- Security of the State
- Public order

**Mandatory SDF obligations:**

**(a) Data Protection Officer (DPO)** — Must:
- Be an **individual** (not a corporate entity)
- Be **based in India**
- Be responsible to the **Board of Directors** (or equivalent governing body)
- Serve as the **point of contact for grievance redressal** under the Act

**(b) Independent Data Auditor** — Must:
- Be independent of the SDF
- Evaluate the SDF's compliance with the Act
- Submit audit report (standards and qualifications prescribed by Rules)

**(c) Other Mandatory Measures:**
1. **Periodic Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIA)** — comprising:
   - Description of rights of Data Principals and purposes of processing
   - Assessment and management of risk to the rights of Data Principals
2. **Periodic audits** (over and above independent audit)
3. **Algorithmic accountability** measures (for automated decisions affecting Data Principals)
4. Any other measure prescribed by the Central Government

> **Penalty for SDF non-compliance**: Up to **₹150 crore**.

---

### Section 11 — Right to Information

Data Principal has the right to obtain from the Data Fiduciary:

1. **Confirmation** of whether personal data is being processed
2. **Summary** of personal data being processed and processing activities
3. **Identities** of Data Fiduciaries and Data Processors with whom personal data has been shared — along with the categories of personal data shared

Data Fiduciary must respond in the manner prescribed by Rules.

---

### Section 12 — Right to Correction and Erasure

Data Principal has the right to:

**(1) Correction, Completion, Updating and Erasure** of personal data for which consent was given (including legitimate use under Section 7(a))

**(2) Correction workflow** — on receiving a request, Data Fiduciary must:
- **(a)** Correct inaccurate or misleading personal data
- **(b)** Complete incomplete personal data
- **(c)** Update personal data

**(3) Erasure workflow** — on receiving an erasure request:
- Erase the personal data
- UNLESS retention is necessary under any law for the time being in force

**Cross-notification**: If DF has shared personal data with other DFs or Processors, it must ensure corrections/erasures are communicated to them.

---

### Section 13 — Right of Grievance Redressal

- Every Data Fiduciary must have a **grievance redressal mechanism**
- Data Principal can raise a grievance with the Data Fiduciary
- If the response is unsatisfactory → Data Principal can complain to the **Board**
- **Prerequisite**: Data Principal must **first exhaust DF's grievance mechanism** before approaching the Board

---

### Section 14 — Right to Nominate

- Data Principal may nominate another individual to exercise rights on their behalf
- Effective on: **death or incapacity** of the Data Principal
- Form and manner of nomination prescribed by Rules

---

## CHAPTER III — RIGHTS AND DUTIES OF DATA PRINCIPAL (Section 15)

### Section 15 — Duties of Data Principal

A Data Principal must NOT:
1. Register a **false or frivolous grievance** or complaint with the Data Fiduciary or Board
2. **Impersonate** another person while providing personal data
3. **Suppress material information** while providing personal data for any document, unique identifier, proof of identity, or proof of address
4. Provide any **false particulars** or suppress any information which she is legally required to disclose

> **Penalty**: Central Government may prescribe penalties for Data Principal violations.

---

## CHAPTER IV — SPECIAL PROVISIONS (Sections 16–17)

### Section 16 — Restriction on Transfer of Personal Data Outside India

- Central Government may **restrict** transfer of personal data to specific countries/territories by **notification**
- **Approach**: Blacklist (not whitelist) — transfers are permitted unless a destination is specifically restricted
- Restrictions do not override higher protection available under other Indian laws

> **Compliance note**: At commencement, no countries are restricted — monitor MeitY notifications. Once a country is notified as restricted, all data transfers to that country must stop.

---

### Section 17 — Exemptions

**Full or partial exemption** from Chapter II (DF obligations), Chapter III (DP rights), and Section 16:

**(1) Blanket exemptions** — Chapter II (except Sections 8(1) and 8(5)), Chapter III, and Section 16 do NOT apply where processing is:
- **(a)** Necessary for **enforcing legal rights or claims**
- **(b)** By a **court, tribunal, or regulatory/supervisory body** for judicial/quasi-judicial/regulatory functions
- **(c)** For **prevention, detection, investigation of offences** or **national security**
- **(d)** Research, archiving or statistics — IF:
  - Data not used for decisions specific to an individual, AND
  - Standards prescribed by Central Government are followed

**(2) Startup exemptions** — Central Government may notify startup Data Fiduciaries as exempt from:
- Section 5 (Notice)
- Section 8(3) (Data accuracy)
- Section 8(7) (Storage limitation)
- Section 10 (SDF obligations)
- Section 11 (Right to information)

**"Startup" definition** (for DPDP purposes):
- Private limited company, partnership, or LLP
- Incorporated in India
- Eligible for and recognised under DPIIT startup recognition criteria

> **Critical**: Even startups must still comply with: Section 4 (lawful processing), Section 6 (consent), Section 8(5) (security), Section 8(6) (breach notification), Section 9 (children's data), and Section 15 (DP duties).

**(3) State/Government exemptions** — Central Government may exempt its own instrumentalities from provisions with additional safeguards for national security, sovereignty, public order.

## CHAPTER V — DATA PROTECTION BOARD OF INDIA (Sections 18–25)

### Section 18 — Establishment of the Board

- **Name**: Data Protection Board of India (DPBI)
- **Established by**: Central Government
- **Nature**: Digital-first regulator — all proceedings conducted digitally
- **Legal status**: Body corporate — can sue and be sued
- **Digital Office**: Board operates through an online platform for end-to-end digital proceedings

---

### Sections 19–20 — Composition, Appointment, Terms

- **Chairperson + Members** — number and qualifications prescribed by Central Government
- Chairperson must be person of ability, integrity, and standing
- Appointment by Central Government after search/selection process prescribed by Rules
- **Salary and terms**: As prescribed
- **Tenure**: As prescribed (not permanent civil servants)

---

### Section 21 — Disqualification

A person is disqualified for Board appointment if they:
- Have been adjudged insolvent
- Have been convicted of an offence involving moral turpitude
- Have become physically or mentally incapable
- Have financial or other interest likely to affect their functions prejudicially
- Have abused their position to the detriment of public interest

---

### Sections 22–25 — Board Administration

- **Resignation**: Notice in writing to Central Government; effective after 3 months or Government acceptance
- **Authentication**: Orders/instruments authenticated per prescribed manner
- **Officers and employees**: Appointed on prescribed terms
- **Powers of Chairperson**: General superintendence, allocation of proceedings, authorisation of officers

---

## CHAPTER VI — POWERS, FUNCTIONS AND PROCEDURES (Sections 26–28)

### Section 27 — Powers and Functions of the Board

The Board shall:

1. On **breach intimation** from a DF → direct urgent remedial/mitigation measures → inquire → impose penalty

2. On **complaint by Data Principal** re breach or non-observance of DF obligations → inquire → impose penalty

3. **Refer disputes to ADR** (Alternate Dispute Resolution) — mediation, conciliation

4. Accept **voluntary undertakings** from persons agreeing to comply

5. Perform any other functions prescribed

---

### Section 28 — Inquiry Procedure

**(1)–(2)** Board may initiate inquiry:
- On its own motion (suo motu)
- On Board's reference
- On complaint by Data Principal (after exhausting DF grievance mechanism)

**(3)** Board determines if **sufficient grounds** exist to proceed

**(4)** If insufficient grounds → Board may close proceedings (with written reasons)

**(5)** If sufficient grounds → Board may inquire into affairs of any person

**(6)** Inquiry must follow **principles of natural justice** — Board must record reasons

**(7) Powers of Civil Court** (Code of Civil Procedure, 1908):
- Summon and examine persons on oath
- Require discovery and production of documents
- Inspect data, books, documents, registers, accounts
- Commission examination of witnesses
- Other prescribed powers

---

## CHAPTER VII — APPELLATE TRIBUNAL (Sections 29–32)

### Section 29 — Appeals

- Appeals against **Board orders** go to **TDSAT**
- Must be filed within **60 days** of Board order (Board may condone delay for sufficient cause)
- Appeal filed with prescribed form, manner, and fee
- TDSAT shall try to dispose of appeal within **6 months**; must record reasons if it cannot
- TDSAT applies its existing procedural rules + prescribed procedure for DPDP appeals

### Section 30 — Orders of Appellate Tribunal

- TDSAT may confirm, modify, or set aside Board orders
- TDSAT orders are **executable as decrees** of civil courts
- Copies of every order sent to Board and parties

### Section 31 — Voluntary Undertaking

- At any stage of proceedings, a person may offer a voluntary undertaking
- Board may accept with conditions
- If Board accepts → proceedings dropped
- If person breaches the undertaking → penalty as if original violation proven

### Section 32 — Alternate Dispute Resolution

- Board may refer matters to ADR (conciliation/mediation) at any stage
- ADR facilitator prescribed by Rules

---

## CHAPTER VIII — PENALTIES (Section 33 + Schedule)

### Section 33 — Imposition of Monetary Penalty

**(1)** The Board may, after inquiry and giving opportunity to be heard, impose monetary penalty per the Schedule.

**(2) Factors for determining penalty amount:**
- Nature, gravity and duration of the breach
- Type and nature of personal data affected (more sensitive = higher penalty)
- **Repetitive nature** (repeated violations → higher penalty)
- Whether person realised gain or avoided loss
- Mitigation actions taken and their timeliness/effectiveness
- Proportionality and deterrence (need to deter future violations)
- Likely impact of penalty on the person

**Section 34 — Destination of Penalties:**
All penalties collected go to the **Consolidated Fund of India** (not to the complainant).

---

### PENALTY SCHEDULE — Complete Table

| Item | Violation Description | Section | Maximum Penalty |
|------|----------------------|---------|----------------|
| **1** | Breach of obligation to protect personal data — failure to implement appropriate security safeguards | Section 8(5) | **₹250 Crore** |
| **2** | Failure to notify the Board and affected Data Principals of a personal data breach | Section 8(6) | **₹200 Crore** |
| **3** | Non-compliance with provisions for processing children's personal data | Section 9 | **₹200 Crore** |
| **4** | Non-compliance with additional obligations of Significant Data Fiduciary | Section 10 | **₹150 Crore** |
| **5** | Non-compliance with general obligations of Data Fiduciary | Section 8 (other sub-sections) | **₹50 Crore** |
| **6** | Non-compliance with duties to respond to Data Principal rights requests | Sections 11–14 | **₹10,000 per instance** |
| **7** | Breach of a voluntary undertaking given to the Board | Section 31 | Up to prescribed amount |

> **Note**: These are **MAXIMUM** penalties. The Board will consider proportionality factors. A small startup's first violation may attract a much lower penalty. Repeat/wilful violations by large DFs will attract the maximum.

## CHAPTER IX — MISCELLANEOUS (Sections 35–44)

### Section 35 — Power to Issue Directions
Central Government may issue directions to the Board in public interest.

### Section 36 — Power to Call for Information
Central Government may call for any information from the Board.

### Section 37 — Protection of Action in Good Faith
Board members/officers protected for good faith actions under the Act.

### Section 38 — Consistency with Other Laws
- DPDP Act is **in addition to** (not in derogation of) other laws
- In case of **conflict** between DPDP and another law → **DPDP Act prevails** to the extent of conflict
- Exception: Laws providing **higher protection** for personal data → those higher standards continue

### Section 39 — Bar of Jurisdiction
No civil court shall have jurisdiction over matters the Board is empowered to handle.

### Section 40 — Rule-Making Powers
Central Government may make rules for all prescribed matters including:
- Notice format and manner (Rule 3)
- Consent Manager registration (Rule 5)
- Security safeguards standards (Rule 6)
- Breach notification procedure (Rule 7)
- Children's data verification (Rule 8)
- SDF obligations and DPIA (Rule 9)
- Data Principal rights procedures (Rule 10)
- Board composition, appointment, procedures
- Penalty procedures
- Appeals (TDSAT) procedure

### Section 41 — Laying of Rules Before Parliament
All rules made under the Act must be laid before both Houses of Parliament.

### Section 42 — Power to Remove Difficulties
Central Government may make orders to remove difficulties in implementation.

### Section 43 — Savings
Anything done under previous law (IT Act 2000) that is consistent with this Act → remains valid.

### Section 44 — Amendments to Other Laws
- Amends the **Right to Information Act, 2005** (Section 8(1)(j)) — exempts personal data from RTI disclosure under DPDP framework
- Amends the **IT Act, 2000** — Section 43A (body corporate data protection liability) repealed as DPDP now governs this
