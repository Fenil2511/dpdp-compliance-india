## PENALTY FACTORS — HOW BOARD WILL CALCULATE

The Board must consider ALL of the following (Section 33(2)):

### Factor 1: Nature, Gravity and Duration
| Gravity Level | Likely Penalty Range |
|--------------|---------------------|
| Minor / Isolated / Short duration | 5–15% of maximum |
| Moderate / Systemic / Extended | 20–50% of maximum |
| Severe / Deliberate / Long-running | 60–100% of maximum |

### Factor 2: Type of Personal Data Affected
| Data Type | Sensitivity Weight |
|-----------|------------------|
| Name, email, phone only | Low — lower penalty |
| Location, financial data | Medium — moderate penalty |
| Health, biometric, children's data | High — higher penalty |
| Multiple sensitive categories | Maximum penalty likely |

### Factor 3: Repetitive Nature
- **First violation**: Base penalty
- **Repeated violation** (same type): Significant uplift (likely 2–3x base)
- **Systemic / Pattern of non-compliance**: Maximum penalty likely + Board directive for structural changes

### Factor 4: Gain / Avoided Loss
- If the violating entity **profited** from the breach or non-compliance → maximum penalty likely
- If no financial gain → lower penalty likely

### Factor 5: Mitigation Actions
**Reduces penalty:**
- Immediately contained the breach
- Notified Board and Data Principals quickly
- Cooperated fully with Board investigation
- Self-reported the violation
- Already remediated by time of hearing

**Increases penalty:**
- Delayed notification
- Failed to contain breach
- Obstructed Board investigation
- Did not remediate

### Factor 6: Proportionality
- Board must consider the **financial capacity** of the entity
- A ₹250 crore fine on a startup may be disproportionate vs. a large conglomerate
- Penalty must be **effective and deterrent** — not merely theoretical

### Factor 7: Impact on the Organisation
- Likely impact on business continuity
- Impact on employees and other stakeholders
- Whether penalty would cause disproportionate harm

## BUSINESS IMPACT ANALYSIS — PENALTY SCENARIOS

### Scenario A: Large Fintech (Series C, ₹500 Cr annual revenue)

**Incident**: Ransomware attack encrypts customer database. 2 million customer records (name, phone, masked card number) affected. DF delays Board notification by 3 weeks.

| Violation | Maximum Penalty |
|-----------|----------------|
| Inadequate security safeguards (Section 8(5)) | ₹250 Crore |
| Failure to notify Board promptly | ₹200 Crore |
| Failure to notify Data Principals | ₹200 Crore |
| **Total Exposure** | **₹650 Crore** |

**Realistic penalty (with some mitigation)**: ₹100–200 crore range

**Business impact beyond penalty**:
- RBI may scrutinise IT framework compliance → operational disruption
- SEBI may review if listed entity → disclosure obligations
- Reputational damage → customer churn
- Cost of breach remediation, forensics, legal → ₹20–50 crore additional

---

### Scenario B: Ed-Tech Platform (10 Lakh student users, 40% under 18)

**Incident**: Platform found to be collecting children's data without verifiable parental consent. Also found to be using behavioural data for personalised advertising to children.

| Violation | Maximum Penalty |
|-----------|----------------|
| Processing children's data without parental consent | ₹200 Crore |
| Targeted advertising directed at children | ₹200 Crore |
| **Total Exposure** | **₹400 Crore** |

**Realistic penalty**: ₹50–150 crore range (first violation, partial mitigation)

---

### Scenario C: Mid-Size NBFC (1 Lakh customers)

**Incident**: Customer requests erasure of data. NBFC ignores 200 such requests over 6 months (focused only on regulatory data retention, not DPDP compliance).

| Violation | Instances | Maximum Penalty |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| Failure to respond to erasure requests | 200 instances | 200 × ₹10,000 = **₹20 Lakh** |

**Compliance note**: Low per-instance penalty, but multiplies rapidly. Also triggers grievances → Board complaints → reputational damage → possible RBI attention.

---

### Scenario D: IT Services Company (Data Processor)

**Incident**: Cloud services company (acting as Data Processor for 50 Indian corporate clients) has a data breach. Client personal data exposed. Company fails to immediately notify any of its clients.

| Violation | Maximum Penalty |
|-----------|----------------|
| Security safeguards failure | ₹250 Crore |
| Breach notification failure (not notifying DF clients) | ₹200 Crore |
| **Total Exposure** | **₹450 Crore** |

**Additional exposure**: Contractual liability to all 50 clients under DPA terms.

## PENALTY REDUCTION STRATEGIES

### Strategy 1: Self-Reporting
If you discover a violation before the Board does:
- Self-report to the Board proactively
- Shows good faith → significant penalty reduction likely
- Allows you to define the narrative

### Strategy 2: Rapid Remediation
- Fix the gap before the Board completes its inquiry
- Document all remediation steps with timestamps
- Demonstrate systemic fixes, not just one-off patches

### Strategy 3: Voluntary Undertaking
Under Section 31 of the Act:
- Offer a voluntary undertaking to the Board
- Commit to specific compliance milestones with deadlines
- Board may close proceedings in exchange for undertaking
- **Risk**: If you breach the undertaking → maximum penalty applicable

### Strategy 4: Full Cooperation with Board
- Respond to all Board requests promptly
- Provide complete, accurate information
- Do not obstruct or delay
- Cooperating entities receive lower penalties in every regulatory system worldwide

### Strategy 5: Pre-emptive Compliance Programme
- Documented, Board-verifiable compliance programme
- Shows the breach was an isolated incident, not systemic non-compliance
- DPIA and audit evidence → demonstrates commitment to compliance

## PRIORITY ACTION MATRIX

Based on penalty exposure, prioritise compliance actions:

### 🔴 Do Immediately (Within 30 Days) — ₹200–250 Cr Exposure
1. Implement/verify encryption of all personal data at rest and in transit
2. Implement breach detection and notification procedures (Rule 7)
3. If you process children's data → implement age verification NOW
4. Verify access controls and logging are in place

### 🟠 Do Within 60 Days — ₹50–200 Cr Exposure
5. Review and update consent notices (Rule 3 compliance)
6. Verify all Data Processor agreements have DPDP clauses
7. Establish internal breach response team and SOP
8. Appoint DPO or Privacy Lead (DPO mandatory for SDFs)

### 🟡 Do Within 90 Days — ₹50 Cr Exposure
9. Complete data inventory and data flow mapping
10. Implement storage limitation / data deletion workflows
11. Update Privacy Policy
12. Conduct staff training on DPDP
13. Monitor MeitY for cross-border transfer restrictions

### 🟢 Do Within 6 Months — ₹10,000/Instance Exposure
14. Build data principal rights request portal
15. Train customer service teams on rights handling
16. Set up grievance officer and publish contact details
17. Implement rights request tracking and SLA management
